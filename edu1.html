Menstruation is an inevitable part of a woman's life. However, in third world countries such as Africa and India, young girls and women face problems of poor hygiene, due to many overlying factors, such as lack of water, limited economic resources, and the lack of cultural acceptance to alternative forms of self-care. At locations like these, the risk of health problems is high, and intervention is a necessity. Instead of being a routine occurrence, it becomes a burden. This article addresses this issue, and ways to help these women.

Attention is finally being given to girls in third world countries, and the problems that they are facing regarding periods. It has been proven that a majority of girls drop out of school after they start their menstrual cycle, due to a lack of proper bathrooms, and sanitation supplies. This compounds to an overall sense of embarrassment. Furthermore, many of the girls are not informed about this biological process, and are left to the bounds of their own imagination. For instance, some girls in rural India believe that they have been injured, or taken over by evil spirits. Their lack of knowledge and the lack of care has prevented them from reaching their potential, increasing the already large gap between men and women.

Especially when the health of girls all over the world depends on it. These young adolescents are mislead with information that has been contaminated by the beliefs of their society. Good or bad, they do not always have access to the truth. In some cases, they are not allowed to be open about their situation, left to fend for themselves. Without the proper knowledge, these girls can fall victim to health risks and more.
